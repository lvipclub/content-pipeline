### Topic: IAQ Monitoring in Commercial Buildings
### Audience: Specifiers, consultants, facility managers

---

Continuous IAQ monitoring is now standard in Class A office buildings across New York, Los Angeles, and Singapore — not because regulations mandated it, but because tenants now ask for air quality data in lease negotiations. For specifiers, this shifts the question from "should we monitor?" to "which parameters and how granular?"

Demand-controlled ventilation based on real-time CO₂ and occupancy sensing is the fastest path to both compliance and energy savings. ASHRAE 62.1-2022 Addendum ab now explicitly permits CO₂-based DCV when sensors are placed in the breathing zone — and the energy savings can offset sensor costs within 18 months in high-occupancy spaces [ASHRAE 62.1-2022 ab, 2023].

Three parameters that facility managers consistently undervalue:
- **CO₂** — the cheapest proxy for ventilation adequacy; a 400 ppm rise above outdoor ambient correlates with a 15% drop in cognitive function scores [Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, 2015]
- **PM2.5** — often worse indoors than out in urban offices with unfiltered make-up air
- **TVOC** — off-gassing from new furniture and finishes persists for 6-12 months post-fitout

For specifiers drafting performance briefs: specify POE (Power over Ethernet) sensors with open protocol output (BACnet or Modbus). Avoid proprietary wireless ecosystems that lock the building into one vendor's cloud dashboard. The sensor hardware is commodity; the integration is where the value lives.

**Cross-border note:** Hong Kong's BEAM Plus and Singapore's Green Mark now align IAQ credits with WELL v2 — meaning the same sensor spec can serve projects from Kowloon to Kuala Lumpur. Dubai is following with Al Sa'fat requirements.
